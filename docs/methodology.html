Methodology

How the macro and risk regime classifications are calculated

Macro Regime

How it works

Three binary signals are computed each month. Each signal scores 1 (positive) or 0 (negative). The scores are summed to produce a macro score from 0 to 3.

ScoreRegime
2 or 3Expansion
0 or 1Contraction

Industrial Production (YoY) INDPRO

Signal ON when: YoY % change > 0

The Federal Reserve's index of real output from manufacturing, mining, and utilities. It's one of the broadest measures of the physical economy's output.

Why it matters: When industrial production is growing year-over-year, it indicates that the real economy is expanding. Declining production often leads or coincides with recessions, and is one of the four indicators used by the NBER to date business cycles.

Chicago Fed National Activity Index CFNAI

Signal ON when: CFNAI > 0

A weighted average of 85 monthly indicators of national economic activity. The index is designed so that zero represents trend growth and positive values indicate above-trend growth.

Why it matters: Because it aggregates 85 indicators across production, employment, consumption, and housing, it provides a single comprehensive gauge of the economy. Persistent negative readings have historically preceded every recession since its inception.

Yield Curve Spread (10Y − 3M) DGS10, DGS3MO

Signal ON when: spread > 0 (curve is normal/positive)

The difference between the 10-Year Treasury yield and the 3-Month Treasury yield, expressed in basis points. A positive spread means long-term rates exceed short-term rates (normal conditions).

Why it matters: An inverted yield curve (negative spread) has preceded every U.S. recession since 1960 with very few false signals. It reflects market expectations that the Fed will need to cut rates in the future due to economic weakness. It's widely regarded as one of the most reliable recession indicators.

Risk Regime

How it works

Three market-based signals are evaluated. The trend signal must be on, and zero stress signals must be firing, for risk to be fully "On."

ConditionRegime
Trend on AND 0 off-votesOn (risk-on)
2 off-votesOff (risk-off)
Everything elseMixed

S&P 500 Trend SP500

Favorable when: S&P 500 ≥ 10-month simple moving average

The S&P 500 index level compared to its trailing 10-month simple moving average (SMA). This is a classic trend-following signal.

Why it matters: When the market is above its long-term moving average, the prevailing trend is up — historically associated with positive forward returns. When it breaks below, it often signals the start of a sustained downturn. The 10-month SMA (roughly a 200-day moving average) filters out short-term noise while capturing meaningful trend changes.

High-Yield Credit OAS BAMLH0A0HYM2

Stress when: HY OAS > max(500 bps, 12-month SMA)

The option-adjusted spread of the ICE BofA High Yield index — the extra yield investors demand to hold high-yield (junk) bonds over risk-free Treasuries, measured in basis points.

Why it matters: Credit spreads are one of the market's most sensitive real-time measures of financial stress. Widening spreads mean investors are demanding more compensation for default risk, which typically occurs during economic slowdowns, liquidity crises, or market panics. The dual threshold (absolute 500 bps floor + rising above its own 12-month SMA) catches both sudden spikes and gradual deterioration.

VIX (Volatility Index) VIXCLS

Stress when: VIX > 25

The CBOE Volatility Index, which measures the market's expectation of 30-day forward volatility implied by S&P 500 option prices. Often called the market's "fear gauge."

Why it matters: Elevated VIX readings indicate that options traders expect large price swings ahead, which is typically associated with uncertainty, risk aversion, and market drawdowns. Readings above 25 historically correspond to periods of meaningful market stress, corrections, or crises. It tends to spike quickly during sell-offs, making it a useful real-time warning signal.

Supporting Metrics (displayed on dashboard)

Unemployment Rate UNRATE

The percentage of the labor force that is unemployed and actively seeking work, reported monthly by the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

Why it matters: Unemployment is a lagging indicator — it tends to rise after a recession has already begun and fall well after recovery is underway. However, the rate of change is informative: a rapid increase in unemployment (the "Sahm Rule") has reliably signaled the onset of recessions.

CPI (Consumer Price Index) CPIAUCSL

A measure of the average change in prices paid by urban consumers for a basket of goods and services, reported monthly. The dashboard shows YoY % change (headline inflation).

Why it matters: Inflation directly influences Fed policy. Persistently high inflation leads to rate hikes, which tighten financial conditions and can slow growth. Deflation signals weak demand. Both extremes create challenging environments for risk assets.

Real GDP (QoQ SAAR) A191RL1Q225SBEA

The quarterly change in real gross domestic product at a seasonally adjusted annual rate. This is the headline GDP growth number reported by the BEA.

Why it matters: GDP is the broadest measure of economic output. Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth is the commonly cited (though unofficial) definition of a recession. Because it's quarterly and heavily revised, it's less useful as a real-time signal, but provides important context for the overall economic picture.

Michigan Consumer Sentiment UMCSENT

The University of Michigan's survey-based index of consumer confidence, covering personal finances, business conditions, and buying conditions.

Why it matters: Consumer spending drives roughly 70% of U.S. GDP. When consumers feel pessimistic, they tend to pull back on spending, which can become self-fulfilling. Sharp drops in sentiment have preceded several recessions, though the relationship is noisy.

Housing Starts HOUST

The number of new residential construction projects begun during a given month, reported at a seasonally adjusted annual rate (SAAR).

Why it matters: Housing is one of the most interest-rate-sensitive sectors of the economy. Declining housing starts often signal that higher rates are biting, and housing downturns have preceded many recessions. Residential investment is a leading indicator in the NBER framework.

Data Notes

  • All data is sourced from FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data).
  • Daily series (S&P 500, VIX, yields, credit spreads) are converted to monthly by taking the median value for each month.
  • Quarterly GDP is repeated across all three months of each quarter.
  • Missing monthly values are filled via linear interpolation and edge-filling to ensure a complete time series.
  • Data is refreshed automatically on the 2nd of each month via GitHub Actions.
  • History begins January 2020.
